H/AI Primitive Language

Volume III
Behavioral Primitives

Behavioral primitives describe how intelligent systems reason, plan, remember, prioritize, and act while interacting with people.

Draft v0.1Volume IIIBehavioral StructureUnder active development

Overview

The behavioral family is the third branch of the H/AI Primitive Language. Temporal primitives describe how interaction unfolds across time. Information primitives describe how meaning moves across representations. Behavioral primitives describe how systems decide what to do.

This portion of the framework remains under active development. The terms below identify the emerging behavioral vocabulary already visible in Human–AI interaction systems that clarify intent, resolve ambiguity, manage constraints, remember context, and select actions.

Primitive Definitions — Draft

Goal

The desired outcome toward which interaction or system behavior is directed.

Constraint

A condition limiting which actions, outputs, or interpretations are acceptable.

Memory

Information retained from prior interaction and made available to future behavior.

Inference

The derivation of unstated meaning, missing information, or likely next action from available context.

Planning

The organization of future steps required to reach a goal under known constraints.

Preference

A user, system, or context-specific tendency that influences action selection.

Agency

The degree to which a system may initiate, recommend, defer, or execute action.

Policy

A rule governing allowable behavior, escalation, refusal, or external consequence.

Example: Missing Information in an Order

A customer gives an order that appears complete but lacks a required preparation detail. The system infers that the missing detail must be resolved before committing the order.

Customer Input

    "I'll have a bagel with whitefish salad and a large coffee."

        ↓

Intent

    Place food order.

        ↓

Constraint

    Bagel order requires preparation state:
        toasted / not toasted

        ↓

Inference

    Required information is missing.

        ↓

Planning

    Ask clarification before order commitment.

        ↓

Action

    "Do you want the bagel toasted?"

Behavioral Relationship to Temporal Primitives

Behavioral primitives frequently determine when a Traversal Point becomes a Commitment Point. A system may continue asking clarifying questions until required constraints are satisfied. Once satisfied, the interaction can safely produce external consequence.

Traversal Point

    State:
        Order pending

    Constraint:
        Toasting preference missing

        ↓

Clarification

        ↓

Traversal Point

    State:
        Toasting preference resolved

        ↓

Commitment Point

    External Action:
        Submit order to kitchen

Behavioral Relationship to Information Primitives

Behavioral reasoning depends on information that may arrive through multiple modalities and representations. A system may infer intent from speech, visual context, gesture, text, memory, or external data.

Information

    Speech:
        "Make it like last time."

    Memory:
        Previous order included toasted sesame bagel.

    Context:
        Restaurant ordering domain.

        ↓

Inference

    User likely wants prior preparation preference.

        ↓

Policy

    Confirm before commitment.

        ↓

Action

    "Last time you had it toasted on sesame. Should I use that again?"

Behavioral Principle

Human–AI systems should not merely produce responses. They should reason about goals, constraints, memory, policy, and consequence before acting. Intelligent behavior emerges when action selection is grounded in context and constrained by human intent.
